# **Chapter 19 — The Critical Point**

**1978–1979**

The system stops responding.

Not because it does not try.
Because it no longer can.

Accumulated tensions reach a different level.

They are no longer pressure.
They are movement.

What was once fragmented
is now visible.

Massive.
Directed.
Coherent in its trajectory.

That is the critical point.

The system no longer faces isolated events.
It faces a dynamic that exceeds it.

Protests are not new.

The difference is their scale.
And their continuity.

They do not disappear.
They repeat.
They intensify.
They expand.

The system responds as it has before.

Control.
Containment.
Reaction.

But this time,
the response does not work.

Because the context has changed.

Control no longer reduces pressure.
It amplifies it.

That is the rupture.

The system’s primary mechanism
ceases to be effective.

Here, another element emerges.

Collective perception.

The system is no longer seen as a functional structure.
It is seen as an obstacle.

And when a system is perceived as an obstacle,
the dynamic changes completely.

Adjustment is no longer the objective.
Replacement is.

That is the irreversible moment.

Rupture ceases to be potential.
It becomes active.

The system attempts to reorganize.

It makes concessions.
It adjusts decisions.
It changes actors.

But it does so too late.

And when response comes too late,
it loses impact.

Here, the central element appears.

The loss of control over the process.

The system no longer defines the pace.
It reacts to it.

And when a system reacts instead of defining,
it has lost its position.

That is the point of no return.

Tensions can no longer be contained.
The structure can no longer sustain them.

Equilibrium disappears.

And when equilibrium disappears,
the system enters a forced transition.

As Charles Kurzman notes,
the Iranian Revolution was not predictable
in its exact form,
but it was the result of an accumulation
of structural factors
that converged at a critical point.¹

This is fundamental.

The system does not collapse by surprise.
It collapses by accumulation.

And at that moment,
continuity is no longer sufficient.

Change is required.

Not adjustment.
Replacement.

That is the critical point.

Not the end.
The beginning of rupture.

---

¹ Kurzman, Charles. *The Unthinkable Revolution in Iran*. Cambridge: Harvard University Press, 2004.
