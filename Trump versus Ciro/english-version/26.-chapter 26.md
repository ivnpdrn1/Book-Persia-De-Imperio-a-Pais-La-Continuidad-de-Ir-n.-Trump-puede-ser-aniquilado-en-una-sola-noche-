# **Chapter 26 — From Cooperation to Structural Enmity**

**1979–Present**

The relationship between Iran and Israel was not always conflictual.

That is the first point.

Before 1979,
both systems operated under a different logic.

Not an open alliance.
But a form of strategic cooperation.

Iran, under the Shah,
did not define itself in opposition to Israel.

Israel was not perceived as a structural threat.

The Iranian system operated within a different framework.

Geopolitical.
Functional.

That framework disappears in 1979.

And with it,
the logic of the relationship.

The Revolution does not only redefine the internal system.
It redefines its external positioning.

Here, the central shift occurs.

Israel ceases to be just another actor.
It becomes a structural reference of opposition.

Not as a tactical conflict.
As a defining element of the system.

That is the point.

Enmity does not emerge as a specific reaction.
It becomes integrated into the system’s logic.

This completely changes the nature of the conflict.

It is no longer only territorial.
Nor exclusively strategic.

It is structural.

It becomes part of the system’s identity.

Here, a critical clarification is required.

It has often been claimed
that the Iranian Constitution mandates the destruction of Israel.

That claim is not accurate in strict legal terms.

The Constitution of the Islamic Republic of Iran
does not contain an explicit provision
ordering the destruction of Israel
as a direct legal mandate.

However,
the Iranian political system has developed,
at the level of discourse and ideology,
a sustained position of opposition to the State of Israel,

including statements by influential actors
that question its legitimacy.

That is the precise point.

It is not an explicit constitutional mandate.
But it is a structural position of the system.

This distinction is fundamental.

Because it separates legal norm
from political and discursive practice.

And both operate differently.

Here, another element appears.

The projection of conflict.

The Iranian system does not confront Israel
only in a direct manner.

It operates across multiple levels.

Regional influence.
Indirect alliances.
Non-state actors.

This model is not new.

It is consistent with its historical logic.

Operating within broader systems
without relying exclusively on direct confrontation.

That is the pattern.

The relationship with Israel is not an isolated conflict.

It is an expression of the system.

It defines positioning.
Conditions decisions.
Structures alliances.

But it also has limits.

Here, the most important point emerges.

The system does not necessarily seek
total direct confrontation.

It manages the conflict.

It maintains it within certain margins.

This behavior is consistent with its logic.

Not the immediate elimination of the adversary.
But the prolonged management of tension.

As Trita Parsi notes,
the relationship between Iran and Israel
evolved from pragmatic cooperation prior to 1979
to a deeply rooted strategic rivalry
within the post-revolutionary regional context.¹

This is fundamental.

The conflict cannot be understood as an anomaly.

It is a consequence of the system.

And like any structural consequence,
it does not disappear easily.

It transforms.
It adapts.
It reconfigures.

That is the final point.

It is not a temporary conflict.

It is a structural relationship.

---

¹ Parsi, Trita. *Treacherous Alliance: The Secret Dealings of Israel, Iran, and the United States*. New Haven: Yale University Press, 2007.
