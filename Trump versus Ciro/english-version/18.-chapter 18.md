# **Chapter 18 — The Accumulation of Tensions**

**1970–1978 CE**

Stability begins to fail.

Not immediately.
Progressively.

What had been contained
begins to move.

Tensions no longer remain hidden.
They begin to surface.

Not as total rupture.
As signals.

Small.
Dispersed.
But constant.

That is the shift.

The system no longer fully controls the surface.
It begins to lose control over what is expressed.

Here, the first indication appears.

The multiplication of frictions.

Social.
Economic.
Political.

Not necessarily coordinated.
But aligned in the same direction.

Dislocation.

The model of forced stability
no longer contains with the same effectiveness.

Accumulated tensions find openings.
They organize.
They amplify.

The system attempts to respond.

It increases control.
Reinforces structures.
Intensifies surveillance.

But that response no longer reduces pressure.

It increases it.

That is the critical point.

The mechanism that sustained stability
ceases to function.

And when the mechanism fails,
the structure is exposed.

Here, another key element emerges.

Convergence.

Tensions cease to be isolated.
They begin to connect.

Different groups.
Different interests.
Different motivations.

But a shared perception.

The system does not respond.

That perception redefines collective behavior.

Dissent ceases to be fragmented.
It begins to acquire coherence.

Not perfect.
But sufficient.

The system no longer faces isolated events.
It faces a cumulative dynamic.

Each event reinforces the next.
Each response generates more pressure.
Each attempt at control further reduces legitimacy.

That is the final cycle.

Here, the most important element appears.

The loss of equilibrium.

The system can no longer sustain
control and legitimacy simultaneously.

It must prioritize.

And by prioritizing control,
it loses legitimacy.

And by losing legitimacy,
it requires more control.

That is the point of no return.

Not collapse.
The impossibility of stabilization.

As Ervand Abrahamian notes,
the period preceding the Iranian Revolution
was marked by an accumulation of social, economic,
and political tensions
that converged into a structural crisis.¹

This is fundamental.

The system does not fall because of a single event.
It falls because it can no longer sustain its equilibrium.

Here, the system enters its final phase.

Not because it has disappeared.
Because it has lost the capacity
to sustain itself as it was.

This is the moment before rupture.

When everything still exists.
But can no longer continue in the same way.

---

¹ Abrahamian, Ervand. *Iran Between Two Revolutions*. Princeton: Princeton University Press, 1982.
