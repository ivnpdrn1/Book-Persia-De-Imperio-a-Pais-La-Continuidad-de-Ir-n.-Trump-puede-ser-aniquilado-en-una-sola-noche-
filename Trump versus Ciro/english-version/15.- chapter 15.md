# **Chapter 15 — The Recentralization of Power**

**1925–1941 CE**

The system cannot remain fragmented.

Disarticulation is not sustainable.

Sooner or later,
a response emerges.

Not as a restoration of the past.
As a reconstruction of control.

Here, a new center emerges.

Not from dynastic continuity.
From structural necessity.

Reza Shah.

But once again,
not as the origin of change.

As the expression of the system.

The system does not simply recover power.
It redefines it.

After decades of fragmentation,
the priority is no longer expansion.

It is coherence.

That is the shift.

Power ceases to be distributed.
It is concentrated.

Regional elites lose autonomy.
Local structures are subordinated.

The center reimposes direction.

Here, the key element of this phase appears.

Forced centralization.

Not gradual.
Not negotiated.

Imposed.

The system requires speed.
And speed does not allow consensus.

It allows decision.

The state is rebuilt.

But not as before.

Now it does so under a different model.

Closer to modern structures.
More oriented toward direct control.
More dependent on central authority.

A more defined state apparatus emerges.

Administration.
Military.
Infrastructure.

The territory is reintegrated.

Not through balance.
Through imposition.

That is the cost.

The system recovers its capacity for action.
But reduces its flexibility.

Here, another fundamental element appears.

The secularization of power.

Religion ceases to be the structural axis of the system.

It does not disappear.
But it loses centrality in the organization of the state.

Control is redefined.

It no longer depends on ideological cohesion.
It depends on institutional capacity.

This completely alters the logic.

The Iranian system does not abandon its identity.
But it partially separates it from political power.

This adjustment allows it to operate
in a different environment.

More competitive.
More modern.
More demanding.

But it also introduces new tensions.

Because by reducing the role of religious identity,
the system alters one of its historical mechanisms of cohesion.

And when a system alters its form of cohesion,
it must replace it with another.

In this case,
cohesion is based on the state.

On its capacity to control.
To organize.
To impose order.

This works.

For a time.

The system once again operates as a unit.

It recovers autonomy.
It reduces direct external influence.

But the balance is fragile.

Because it depends on the continuity of control.

And when control is the primary mechanism of cohesion,
any disruption weakens everything.

As Ervand Abrahamian notes,
the period of Reza Shah was characterized
by strong state centralization
and an attempt at accelerated modernization
under authoritarian control.¹

This is fundamental.

The system does not only reorganize.
It transforms.

But that transformation does not eliminate its tensions.

It reorganizes them.

And by reorganizing them,
it prepares the next cycle.

The system has recovered its center.

But now it depends on it more than before.

That is the new balance.

And also,
its new limit.

---

¹ Abrahamian, Ervand. *A History of Modern Iran*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2008.
