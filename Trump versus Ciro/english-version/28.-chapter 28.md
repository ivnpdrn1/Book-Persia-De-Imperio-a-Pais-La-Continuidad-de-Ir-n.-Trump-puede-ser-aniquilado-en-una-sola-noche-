# **Chapter 28 — Iran and International Terrorism: Perception, Structure, and Reality**

**1979–Present**

The term terrorism is not neutral.

It is not only a category.
It is a political definition.

And like any political definition,
it depends on the framework from which it is applied.

That is the first point.

Not to analyze the concept alone.
But to analyze how it is used.

In the Iranian case,
the association with international terrorism
has been a constant in the discourse
of multiple governments,

particularly in the West
and in parts of the regional environment.

This association does not arise from a single event.

It is constructed over time.

Through perceived patterns.
Identified relationships.
And sustained conflicts.

Here, the central element emerges.

The difference between direct action
and indirect influence.

The Iranian system is not primarily defined
by conventional operations beyond its borders.

It is defined by its ability
to operate through networks.

Alliances.
Non-state actors.
Indirect structures.

This model is not unique.

But in this case,
it is structural.

Various actors linked to the region
have been designated by some states
as terrorist organizations,

including Hezbollah
and, in certain contexts, Hamas.¹

The Iranian system has been associated
with political, financial, or logistical support
to these actors.

That is the basis of international perception.

But that perception requires distinction.

Association is not necessarily total control.

Influence is not equivalent
to absolute direction.

That is the point often simplified.

The system does not operate
under a binary logic.

It operates under a network logic.

The capacity to influence.
Not necessarily to control every outcome.

This introduces an additional layer of complexity.

Attribution.

In environments of indirect conflict,
the relationship between action and responsibility
is not always linear.

And that ambiguity is part of the system.

Not as an accident.
As an operational characteristic.

Here, another fundamental element emerges.

Human impact.

The conflict dynamics in which the Iranian system
is perceived as an actor
have generated real consequences.

Victims.
Displacement.
Instability.

This impact is not uniform.
It does not respond to a single cause.

But it is documented
across multiple regional contexts.

At the same time,
the Iranian system itself
has experienced internal costs
associated with its security environment.

Internal tensions.
Episodes of confrontation.
Direct impact on its population
at different moments.

That is the point of balance.

The system is not only projected outward.
It also absorbs pressure inward.

Here, the most important dimension appears.

Global perception.

The Iranian system is not evaluated
only by its actions.

It is evaluated by its position
within the international system.

And that position is defined by conflict.
By competition.
By structural distrust.

As Bruce Hoffman notes,
the concept of international terrorism
and its application
depend largely on political context
and the interpretation of the actors involved.²

This is fundamental.

The analysis cannot be reduced to a label.

It must consider structure.
Relationship.
Context.

Here, the final point emerges.

The Iranian system cannot be understood
solely through the concept of terrorism.

But it also cannot be analyzed
while ignoring that dimension.

It is part of its interaction
with the environment.

Part of its way of projecting influence.

And part of how it is perceived globally.

That is the balance.

Not to deny.
Not to simplify.
To understand.

---

¹ Pollack, Kenneth M. *The Persian Puzzle: The Conflict Between Iran and America*. New York: Random House, 2004.
² Hoffman, Bruce. *Inside Terrorism*. New York: Columbia University Press, 2006.
