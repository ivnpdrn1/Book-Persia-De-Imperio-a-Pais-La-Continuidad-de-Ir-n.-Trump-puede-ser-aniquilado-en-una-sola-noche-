# **Chapter 29 — Iran and the Global Balance: Nuclear Deterrence and Energy Power**

**1979–Present**

Power is not defined solely by military capability.

It is defined by the ability to influence balance.

That is the point.

The Iranian system does not compete only in traditional terms.

It operates across two critical dimensions
of the international system.

Energy.
Deterrence.

And both redefine its position.

---

The first is structural.

The energy market.

Iran is not only a producer.
It is an actor within a highly sensitive global system.

Oil is not an isolated resource.

It is an element of international stability.

Price.
Supply.
Routes.

All respond to balance.

And any disruption to that balance
has immediate impact.

The Iranian system operates within that space.

Not always through direct production.

But through its capacity
to affect the environment.

Geographic position.
Indirect control of routes.
Regional influence.

That is the key element.

The system does not need to dominate the market.

It only needs the ability to alter it.

And that ability generates influence.

Because in an interdependent market,
the possibility of disruption
is a form of power.

---

The second dimension is more complex.

The nuclear program.

Not as a technical fact.
As an element of deterrence.

Here, a fundamental distinction appears.

Capability.
Intent.

They are not equivalent.

The Iranian system has developed nuclear capabilities
within a framework
that has generated sustained concern
in the international system.

That concern does not depend only
on what exists.

It depends on what could exist.

That is the central point of deterrence.

It is not based solely on use.

It is based on possibility.

Here, the key concept emerges.

Strategic ambiguity.

The system does not need to declare an absolute position.

It only needs to maintain uncertainty.

And that uncertainty shapes
the behavior of other actors.

That is the mechanism.

Not direct control.
Indirect influence.

Through the perception of risk.

---

These two dimensions do not operate in isolation.

They combine.
They reinforce each other.

The Iranian system is not defined
only by its energy capacity
or only by its nuclear position.

It is defined by the interaction of both.

Energy creates relevance.
Deterrence creates caution.

Together,
they create room for maneuver.

That is the balance.

The system does not dominate the global order.

But it cannot be ignored.

---

Here, the most important point emerges.

The system does not necessarily seek direct confrontation.

It seeks to avoid absolute vulnerability.

That is the objective of deterrence.

Not to win.
To avoid losing in structural terms.

And in that context,
the capacity to generate uncertainty
becomes a tool.

---

As Suzanne Maloney notes,
Iran’s position in the global energy market
and its nuclear program
have been determining factors
in its relationship
with the contemporary international system.¹

This is fundamental.

The Iranian system is not defined
only by its internal behavior.

It is defined by its impact
on global balance.

And that impact does not depend
on constant action.

It depends on potential capability.

---

Here, the limit appears.

The same elements that generate influence
generate pressure.

Sanctions.
Isolation.
External constraints.

The system absorbs that pressure.

But it does not eliminate it.

That is the real balance.

Influence.
Cost.
Capability.
Constraint.

---

The Iranian system is not a dominant power.

But it is a structural actor.

And a structural actor does not need to dominate.

It needs to remain relevant.

That is the final point.

Global balance is not defined only
by those who control.

It is also defined
by those who can alter.

And within that space,
the Iranian system continues to operate.

---

¹ Maloney, Suzanne. *Iran’s Political Economy since the Revolution*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2015.
