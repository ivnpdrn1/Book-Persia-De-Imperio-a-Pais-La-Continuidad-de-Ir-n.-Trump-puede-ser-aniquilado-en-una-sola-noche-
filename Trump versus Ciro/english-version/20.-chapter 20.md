# **Chapter 20 — Collapse**

**1979**

The system breaks.

Not partially.
Completely.

The structure that had defined power for decades
ceases to exist.

The center disappears.
Control dissolves.
Continuity, in its visible form,
ends.

But the system does not disappear.

That is the point.

The revolution does not eliminate the Iranian system.
It eliminates its previous form.

Here, the deepest shift occurs.

This is not a gradual transition.
It is a structural rupture.

The model based on centralized control,
directed modernization,
and forced stability
collapses.

It can no longer sustain itself.
It can no longer adapt.
It can no longer contain accumulated pressure.

That is the outcome.

The system reaches a point
where it can no longer operate
under its own recent logic.

And when that happens,
rupture becomes inevitable.

Here, a key element emerges.

Speed.

Change is not slow.
It is not progressive.

It is accelerated.

In a short period of time,
a structure that appeared stable
disappears.

Institutions cease to function as before.
Authority loses meaning.

The system enters a vacuum.

But that vacuum is not absence.

It is space for reconstruction.

That is the central point.

Collapse is not the end.

It is the moment when the system loses its form
and begins to search for a new one.

Here, another element appears.

Redefinition.

The system does not attempt to restore what came before.
It builds something different.

With another logic.
With another foundation.
With another form of cohesion.

The revolution is not only political.

It is structural.

It redefines the origin of power.
It redefines the relationship between state and society.
It redefines the way the system sustains itself.

That is the definitive shift.

The system ceases to depend on the previous model.
And constructs a new one.

As Ervand Abrahamian notes,
the Iranian Revolution of 1979
represented not only the collapse of the previous regime,
but the creation of a new political structure
based on entirely different principles.¹

This is fundamental.

The system does not disappear.

It transforms.

That is the pattern that repeats
throughout Iranian history.

Change of form.
Continuity of logic.
Adaptation under pressure.
Reconfiguration after collapse.

Here, a cycle closes.

But not the system.

The system continues.

In another form.
Under another structure.
With another visible logic.

But with the same capacity.

To adapt.
And to endure.

---

¹ Abrahamian, Ervand. *A History of Modern Iran*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2008.
