# **Chapter 14 — The Fragmentation of Sovereignty**

**1907–1925 CE**

Sovereignty does not disappear.

It divides.

What was once external influence
now becomes formalized.

It is organized.
It becomes structure.

The Iranian system no longer operates under a single logic.
It operates under multiple centers of pressure.

That is the shift.

The state is not lost.
Its control is fragmented.

Here, a key moment emerges.

1907.

The agreement between external powers
does not redefine territory.

It redefines power.

Zones of influence.
Areas of indirect control.
Spaces where decision-making is no longer fully Iranian.

The system maintains its form.
But it loses its functional unity.

That is the core of fragmentation.

Not territorial in the classical sense.
Structural.

Power is no longer exercised from a coherent center.
It is distributed among internal and external actors.

And that distribution is not balanced.

It is conditioned.

Here, a new dynamic appears.

Competition within the system.

Not only among local elites.
But among external influences.

Russia.
Great Britain.

Each operates according to its own interests.

And the Iranian system becomes the space
where those interests interact.

That is the critical point.

The system no longer defines its own internal logic.
It becomes the result of multiple external logics.

This completely alters its functioning.

Coherence disappears.

Decisions no longer follow a unified strategy.
They respond to temporary balances.
Immediate pressures.
Imposed necessities.

The system continues to exist.

But it no longer operates as a system.

It operates as a field.

A space where different forces interact
without effective central coordination.

Here, another fundamental element emerges.

Legitimacy.

Central power does not only lose operational control.
It loses credibility.

The perception of authority weakens.

And when legitimacy declines,
the capacity for internal reorganization
declines even further.

The system enters a cycle.

Less control generates more fragmentation.
More fragmentation reduces the ability
to recover control.

This is the lowest point so far.

Not because of destruction.
But because of disarticulation.

The system has not been eliminated.
But it has lost its ability to function as a unit.

As Ervand Abrahamian notes,
foreign intervention and internal weakness
transformed Iran into a state with limited sovereignty
during this period.¹

This is fundamental.

The system does not disappear.
But it ceases to be fully sovereign.

And when a system loses effective sovereignty,
it loses the capacity to redefine itself independently.

That is the maximum limit of this phase.

Not collapse.
The loss of control over its own evolution.

But this state is not permanent.

It is unsustainable.

Because a system cannot operate indefinitely
without internal coherence.

At some point,
it must reorganize.

It must rebuild its center.
It must recover decision-making capacity.

That moment has not yet arrived.

But its necessity is now inevitable.

---

¹ Abrahamian, Ervand. *A History of Modern Iran*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2008.
