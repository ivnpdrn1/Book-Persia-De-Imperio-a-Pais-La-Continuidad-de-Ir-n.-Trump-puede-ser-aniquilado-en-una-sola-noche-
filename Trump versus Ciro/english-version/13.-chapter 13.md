# **Chapter 13 — External Influence**

**1850–1907 CE**

The system no longer defines its environment.

The environment begins to define the system.

Internal weakness does not disappear.
It is exposed.

And once exposed,
it becomes a point of entry.

Here, external powers emerge.

Not as direct invasion.
As progressive influence.

Empires with superior capacity.

Economic.
Military.
Strategic.

Russia.
Great Britain.

They do not seek to replace the Iranian system.
They seek to condition it.

That is the shift.

Control is no longer exercised solely from within.
It is exercised from outside.

Through agreements.
Through concessions.
Through sustained pressure.

The Iranian system maintains its formal structure.
But it loses real decision-making capacity.

That is the core of this stage.

Apparent sovereignty.
Effective dependence.

Economic decisions begin to respond to external interests.
Strategic resources are negotiated under unequal conditions.

The margin for maneuver shrinks.
And with it,
the capacity for autonomous adaptation.

Here, another key element emerges.

Internal fragmentation intensifies.

Elites no longer operate under a common logic.
They respond to different incentives.

And in that context,
external influence finds multiple points of entry.

The system is no longer a coherent block.
It becomes a permeable space.

That is the structural shift.

External pressure does not destroy.
It penetrates.

And by penetrating,
it redefines internal functioning.

Here, a fundamental phenomenon appears.

Intermediation.

The system no longer interacts directly with the global environment.
It does so through external actors
who condition its decisions.

This completely alters the dynamic.

Because it introduces a new variable.

The system no longer fully controls its own evolution.
It shares it.

And when a system shares control over its evolution,
it loses coherence.

Decisions no longer respond to a consistent internal logic.
They begin to respond to multiple pressures.

At times, contradictory.

That is the turning point.

External influence does not only limit.
It disorganizes.

But the system does not disappear.

It attempts to adjust.

It negotiates.
It concedes in some areas.
It resists in others.

But it does so without a unified strategy.

That is the problem.

Adaptation still exists.
But it is no longer fully controlled.

As Abbas Amanat notes,
nineteenth-century Iran experienced growing intervention
by foreign powers
that shaped its political and economic development.¹

This is fundamental.

The system does not lose its form.
It loses its capacity to operate independently.

And that loss is not immediate.
It is progressive.

It accumulates.
It deepens.

Until it becomes a new structural condition.

The system no longer faces only its own limits.
It faces imposed limits.

And when limits are external,
the capacity for adaptation is reduced even further.

That is the point.

The system still exists.
But it no longer fully decides how to do so.

---

¹ Amanat, Abbas. *Iran: A Modern History*. New Haven: Yale University Press, 2017.
