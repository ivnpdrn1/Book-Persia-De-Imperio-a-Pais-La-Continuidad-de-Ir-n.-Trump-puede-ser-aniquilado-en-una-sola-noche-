# **Chapter 27 — The Projection of Conflict: Iran and the Architecture of Global Influence**

**1979–Present**

The system does not operate only within its borders.

It never has.

But in this phase,
that projection takes on a different form.

More complex.
More distributed.
More difficult to interpret.

That is the shift.

Power is no longer expressed solely through the state.

It is expressed through networks.
Through relationships.
Through indirect structures.

Here, the key concept emerges.

Asymmetric projection.

The Iranian system does not compete
on equal terms with major powers.

It does not need to.

It operates on another level.

It builds influence
where direct control is not possible.

That is the model.

Regional alliances.
Support for non-state actors.
Indirect presence across multiple arenas.

Not as classical territorial expansion.
As functional extension of the system.

This generates a perception.

The Iranian system is associated in many international analyses
with activities that include support for armed groups
in different regions.

This association does not emerge from a single source.

It is sustained by multiple governments
and international organizations,

particularly in relation to groups such as Hezbollah,
and in certain contexts, Hamas.¹

That is the point that must be understood precisely.

It is not only about accusations.

It is about a perception
that has been structured over time.

But that perception is also part of the system.

Influence is not measured only by actions.

It is measured by how those actions are interpreted.

And that interpretation conditions
the international response.

Here, a fundamental distinction appears.

Support.
Control.

They are not the same.

The Iranian system does not necessarily operate
through direct control over all actors
with which it is associated.

But it has developed relationships
that allow it to project influence.

That is the model.

Not absolute dominance.

The capacity to shape outcomes.

This behavior is not new.

It is consistent with its historical logic.

Operating within broader systems.
Adapting to them.
Influencing from within.

But in the current context,
that logic is perceived as a threat.

That is the critical point.

The system does not change its essential behavior.

The environment in which that behavior is interpreted changes.

And that environment is more sensitive.

More interconnected.
More immediate.

Here, another element emerges.

Cost.

Asymmetric projection allows influence.

But it also generates response.

Sanctions.
Isolation.
International pressure.

The system absorbs that pressure.

It adjusts.
It continues to operate.

But each adjustment carries cumulative cost.

That is the emerging limit.

The ability to project influence
without generating unsustainable pressure.

As Kenneth M. Pollack notes,
Iran’s regional strategy combines elements
of deterrence, indirect influence,
and asymmetric projection of power.¹

This is fundamental.

The system does not act randomly.

It operates under a logic.

Maximize influence.
Minimize direct exposure.
Maintain adaptive capacity.

That is the balance.

But that balance is not stable.

It depends on the environment.
On pressure.
On external response.

Here, the final point emerges.

The global projection of the Iranian system
is not conventional expansion.

It is an extension of its historical logic.

To adapt.
To integrate.
To influence.

But within an environment
where every action is amplified,
interpreted,
and responded to immediately.

That is the new scenario.

And that scenario redefines
the system’s reach.

---

¹ Pollack, Kenneth M. *The Persian Puzzle: The Conflict Between Iran and America*. New York: Random House, 2004.
